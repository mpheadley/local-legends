In Calhoun County, there's no shortage of churches. Drive any stretch of highway between Anniston and Jacksonville and you'll pass Baptist, Methodist, Presbyterian, Catholic, AME, Church of Christ, nondenominational — the full spectrum of American faith, planted every quarter mile like fence posts. Most of the time, these congregations do their own thing. But since 1975, Interfaith Ministries has been what happens when they decide to do something together.

## A Coalition Born from Common Sense

The story starts simply enough. In 1975, representatives from Protestant churches, Catholic parishes, and Temple Beth El — Anniston's Jewish congregation — sat down together and asked a practical question: why are we all trying to do the same thing separately?

Individual churches had benevolence funds for helping people in crisis. But each fund was small, each process was different, and people in need were bouncing from congregation to congregation hoping someone had money left in the budget that month. The answer was obvious, even if the execution took work: pool the resources, centralize the intake, and let one organization do what dozens of churches couldn't do efficiently on their own.

By 1978, Interfaith Ministries was incorporated as a 501(c)(3). Nearly fifty years later, the model still holds. The congregations keep giving, the doors keep opening, and the mission hasn't changed: bring God's people together to serve God's people in need.

## Sharing a Campus, Sharing a Mission

Interfaith Ministries operates out of Suite A at 1431 Gurnee Avenue in Anniston — on the campus of First United Methodist Church. It's not a coincidence, and it's not just a landlord-tenant arrangement. The two organizations share more than an address. They share volunteers, resources, and a stretch of sidewalk where on any given weekday you'll see the same faces moving between church work and community work, often without drawing much distinction between the two.

First UMC's facility, The Bridge, has hosted Interfaith Ministries' annual conferences and serves as the site for United Way's Warming Station during cold weather. The overlap is deliberate — when a church opens its doors to the community and a nonprofit sets up shop on its campus, the line between sacred space and service space disappears. That's the point.

The relationship is a living example of what Interfaith Ministries was built to do: make cooperation the default instead of the exception.

## The Programs

What surprises most people about Interfaith Ministries is the breadth of what it does. This isn't a single-purpose charity. It's a network of programs, each one addressing a different crack in the safety net.

**Emergency Assistance** is the core — rent payments to prevent eviction, utility bills to keep the lights on, help with prescriptions and transportation costs. The needs are urgent and the math is unforgiving. A family that falls two months behind on rent doesn't need spiritual counsel. They need $800 before Friday.

**The Free Dental Clinic** operates through referrals to the Adult Dental Clinic at RMC West, but screening happens at Interfaith Ministries. Area dentists donate their services, providing emergency extractions to Calhoun County residents who can't afford dental care. The clinic sees patients at 8 a.m. on Wednesdays — a small window, but for someone who's been living with a toothache they can't afford to fix, it's everything.

**Meals on Wheels** delivers hot, nutritious lunches to homebound individuals across the Anniston and Oxford area. A continuous stream of volunteers makes the daily routes possible — people who give up their lunch hour to make sure their neighbors eat.

**The Open Door** serves homeless and displaced individuals with coffee, showers, blankets, sleeping bags, toiletries, and snacks. It's not a shelter. It's a threshold — a place where people who've fallen through every other program can at least get clean, get warm, and be treated like human beings.

**SenioRx** helps seniors and individuals with disabilities navigate the maze of pharmaceutical company assistance programs to get free prescriptions. It's paperwork-heavy and unglamorous, but for someone choosing between medication and groceries, it changes the equation.

**The Christmas Clearing House** coordinates holiday gifts for children in low-income families — the kind of program that runs on donations and the stubborn belief that no kid in Calhoun County should wake up on Christmas morning with nothing.

## The People in the Middle

The staff and volunteers at Interfaith Ministries occupy an unusual position. They sit at the intersection of faith and bureaucracy, compassion and logistics. On any given day, they're reviewing applications, calling landlords, verifying utility accounts, routing dental referrals, loading Meals on Wheels vans, and making decisions about how to allocate funds that are never quite enough to meet the demand.

It's social work dressed in church clothes — or maybe it's ministry dressed in spreadsheets. Either way, the people doing it will tell you the same thing: the paperwork exists to make sure the money goes where it's needed most, not to make people jump through hoops. There's a difference between accountability and gatekeeping, and Interfaith tries to stay on the right side of that line.

The member congregations themselves represent a quiet rebuke to the idea that churches can't work together. Baptist and Catholic, Black and white, downtown and rural, Christian and Jewish — the coalition holds because the shared commitment to helping people outweighs the theological differences that might otherwise keep them in separate lanes.

## What Falls Through the Cracks

Every community has gaps in its safety net. Government programs have eligibility requirements and processing times. Nonprofit organizations have funding cycles and service areas. Churches have limited budgets and volunteer bandwidth. Interfaith Ministries exists in the space between all of these — the place where someone who doesn't quite qualify for one program and can't wait for another can still get help before the situation becomes a crisis.

This is the work that rarely makes the news. There's no ribbon-cutting when a family avoids eviction. No press release when someone's electricity stays on. No headline when a homebound widow gets a hot meal at noon instead of eating crackers alone. The impact is measured in crises averted, in the things that didn't happen because someone intervened at the right moment with the right amount of money and the willingness to act fast.

## Nearly Fifty Years and Counting

Interfaith Ministries of Calhoun County carries a four-star rating on Charity Navigator — a 95% score that puts it in rare company for an organization of its size. That rating reflects what the people in Anniston already know: the money goes where it's supposed to go, the programs do what they say they do, and the doors stay open Monday through Thursday because the need doesn't take a day off.

The churches keep giving. The volunteers keep showing up. The dental clinic keeps seeing patients on Wednesday mornings. The Meals on Wheels vans keep rolling at noon. And on the campus of First United Methodist Church, in a modest suite on Gurnee Avenue, the work continues — not because anyone solved poverty in Calhoun County, but because enough people decided that letting their neighbors fall wasn't an option.

It's not a solution. It's a commitment. And in Calhoun County, that's often the difference between falling and not.
